Neighbourhood Context

Shinjuku, Tokyo

Safety & Health

🟢
Level 1 — Exercise Normal Precautions

Japan is one of the world's safest travel destinations. Violent crime against tourists is extremely rare. Primary concerns are pickpocketing in busy tourist areas and natural disaster preparedness.

Source: US State Department · Updated Feb 2026

🛂 Visa & Entry Requirements (US Citizens)

  • No visa required for stays up to 90 days
  • Passport must be valid for the duration of your stay
  • Return ticket required at border — have your itinerary printed
  • Complete Japan's Visit Japan Web registration before arrival (speeds up customs)

💉 Health Requirements

  • Vaccinations required: None for US citizens
  • Recommended vaccines: Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B (standard travel), updated routine vaccines
  • Water safety: Tap water is safe and excellent quality throughout Japan
  • Medical care: World-class. Major cities have English-speaking hospitals. Keep travel insurance card accessible.
  • Medications: Some common US medications (including certain cold medicines with pseudoephedrine) are controlled in Japan — check MHLW list before packing

🌋 Natural Hazard Awareness

  • Earthquakes: Japan is seismically active. Know drop-cover-hold on and exit procedures. Your hotel will have guidance.
  • April weather: Generally mild. Occasional heavy rain days (pack a compact umbrella). No typhoon season (Aug–Oct).
  • Tsunami zones: Coastal areas may have signs. Know your hotel's evacuation route.

Etiquette & Customs

🙏 Greetings

  • A slight bow (15°) is the everyday greeting — more formal = deeper bow
  • Handshakes are acceptable with Westerners; wait for the Japanese person to initiate
  • Business cards (meishi) are presented and received with both hands — look at it before putting it away

🚇 Public Transport

  • Silence is the norm — phone calls are avoided on trains
  • Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo; right in Osaka/Kyoto
  • Priority seats are for elderly, disabled, and pregnant passengers — not tourists with tired feet

🍜 Dining

  • Say Itadakimasu before eating (receiving food gratefully)
  • Say Gochisousama deshita after eating (thanks for the meal)
  • Do not tip — it is considered rude and confusing
  • Slurping noodles is correct and a genuine compliment
  • Pay at a register, not at the table — many restaurants use ticket machines

⛩️ Temples & Shrines

  • Remove hats before entering temples
  • Remove shoes when indicated (look for the sign or other guests doing so)
  • Purify hands at the temizuya (stone fountain) before approaching a shrine
  • Follow the bell-pull → bow → clap → bow sequence at shrines
  • Photography generally allowed outdoors; check signs inside temples
💡 Tipping culture: Japan does not tip — not in taxis, restaurants, hotels, or anywhere. Attempting to tip can actually cause confusion or offence. Exceptional service is simply the standard.
