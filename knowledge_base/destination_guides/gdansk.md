# Gdansk Travel Guide

Gdańsk (also known by its German name, Danzig) is a city in Poland on the Baltic Sea. Gdańsk is considered one of the most beautiful cities on the Baltic Sea and has magnificent architecture.

Understand
thumb|300px|Długa Street with the Main Town Hall in the background
Gdańsk is the capital of Pomerania. In 2021, it was home to 471,000 people. Gdańsk with nearby Sopot and Gdynia are often referred to as Trójmiasto ("tricity"). 

Its position on the Baltic has made Gdańsk one of the most important port cities in Northern Europe, and the scene of a disturbing past. Gdańsk used to be the biggest and richest city in the Polish Kingdom. The first claim to fame for Danzig (as it was then known) was its membership in the Hanseatic League as an important Baltic port on the crossroads of North East and Central Europe.

World War II was ignited by a dispute over the control of the city. By the end of the war the city lay almost completely in ruins. The German population was expelled and replaced by Poles as the city came under Polish rule and changed its name to Gdańsk. However, the impact of its former German ties are still evident. Most of the old buildings were damaged or destroyed in World War II, but they have been painstakingly restored or rebuilt, although in a style that is not exactly the same as before the war.

In modern history, Gdańsk is known as the birthplace of Solidarity (pl: Solidarność), the labour and democracy movement that helped to bring down the Communist government in Poland at the end of the Cold War. The movement was led by the charismatic leader, Lech Wałęsa, who won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1983 and in 1990 became Poland's first post-Communist president.

By plane

Transport from airport to city

Bus: Bus 210 operates between the airport (bus stop: Port Lotniczy) and Gdańsk, and makes a stop at the main train station (Gdańsk Główny), a 35–50 minute trip. Other than that, the airport is also serviced by bus lines 110 (towards the Wrzeszcz railway station where you can change to a train or a tram), 120 (towards Łostowice Świętokrzyska where you can change to a tram), 122 (towards Sopot Kamienny Potok SKM), and night line N3 (towards the main railway station which is Gdańsk Główny, and the Wrzeszcz railway station). The regular fare is 4,80zł for single travel in one bus on one route. If you need to change to a different route, there is a 6,00zł 75-minute ticket (as of Aug 2025) with which you can use as many buses/trams as you want as long as the ticket is valid.

Perhaps the preferred way to buy ticket is install the Jakdojade public transport app, which provides more information about route and stops in English. It is also possible to buy ticket from a news stand, ticket machine or Bilety shop and from driver. Note that long-term tickets (e.g. monthly tickets) are no longer available in the Jakdojade app, and they have to be purchased via the FALA app. Short-term tickets (valid for up to 3 days) are still available in Jakdojade. 

Make sure you have a correct ticket and that it is validated in the machine inside bus. Ticket inspectors regularly check after few stops on the route, looking for foreign travelers who have the wrong ticket or failed to punch their ticket correctly, either due to forgetting or because of a ticket machine malfunctions etc. The fine for an incorrect ticket is on the order of 200-300 zł. Talk with fellow passengers, driver about validity of ticket if you are not 100% sure.

Train: There is a POLREGIO train connection between the airport (airport stop: Gdańsk Port Lotniczy) and the city. There are a few direct connections to the main train station in Gdańsk, but otherwise, you will need a 30-minute train to Gdańsk Wrzeszcz (in the north of the city), then transfer on to Gdansk Glowny (Main Railway Station) for another 5-10 minutes. Single tickets to the main train station cost 7 zł. The train station is accessible from the west end of Terminal T2 via a covered bridge. Ticket machines are on the platforms.  

Airport shuttle bus: A transport shuttle service to Gdańsk centre costs 9.90 zł. You can buy ticket directly in the bus. This is a direct line between airport and city centre. The bus stop in Gdańsk is located in front of Mercure Hevelius hotel (a 5 minute walk from the Main Railway Station). The journey time depends on traffic, but takes around 25–30 minutes.

Taxi and rideshare services: A taxi from the airport to the city centre should cost up to 100 zł however will cost more in the evenings (after 22:00) or at weekends.

"Neptun" is an officially recognized company. Taxi drivers will be waiting in the arrivals hall offering fixed price transfers. Avoid the unofficial, rip-off "taxis" who approach you as soon as you have cleared security, unless you know how to deal with them. Uber and Bolt operate in the area too and cost is provided upfront.

Private transfers: MPA Poland provides transport services around Tri-City and Pomerania region. An airport transfer from/to airport costs 100 zł up to 8 people.

By train
 thumb | 300px | Gdańsk Główny station, completed in 1900.

Gdańsk is a major hub on the Polish railway network, being by far the largest rail node for northern Poland. High-speed trains runs frequently from Krakow (6 hr) via Warsaw (3 hr) and there are slower intercity trains from most major Polish cities with at least a couple of departures every day. All inter-city trains to Gdańsk continue north up the coast to Sopot and Gdynia, a 25-min ride. Local SKM trains also shuttle on this route every 10 min or so. 

There are a few international trains too. One daily train from Berlin, taking six hours via Frankfurt (Oder), Poznań, Gniezno and Bydgoszcz as well as one daily train from Vienna via Ostrava (for Prague), Katowice and Warsaw, 10 hr 30 min. Since 2024, direct trains (4 times a day) from Prague (called Baltic Express) go there. The ride takes about 8 hr 30 min (10 hr 30 min by a night train).

National operator PKP runs all high-speed and intercity trains while SKM operates the regional trains between Gdańsk, Sopot and Gdynia.

See Poland#Get in for general tips on rail travel. It helps to write down your destination and preferred time to show to ticket clerks as trains have different prices.

Get around

By tram and bus
thumb|20px (2024)

Zarząd Transportu Miejskiego, the City Transportation Office. Easyway journey planner helps to find schedules and routes.

Trams and buses are cheap (3.80 zł/hr) and frequent. Tickets can be bought on board, and also from ticket machines (which are at most popular stops) and kiosks. The most popular website and mobile app for journey planning is jakdojade.pl. Google Maps has reliable public transport data, and is an excellent tool for planning your journeys.

Single journey tickets are valid until leaving the bus or tram. They cost 4.80 zł for a full-price and 2.40 zł for a half-price ticket.

Time tickets are based on travel time, so you must estimate the duration of the journey or have a few tickets extra to validate when your previous ticket expires. Time tickets are either valid for up to 24 hr and allow any amount of journeys and changes within that time. The 75-min time ticket costs 6 zł for a full-price ticket and 3 zł for a half-price ticket. The 24-hr ticket costs 22 zł for a full-price ticket and 11 zł for a half-price ticket and is valid on all trams, buses during the day and on night buses.

All pre-purchased paper tickets need to be validated at the start of the journey. Alternatively you can pay on board with payment cards only (no cash). You don't pay the driver, you go to one of the machines on board. No paper ticket will be printed; rather, your credit card or phone acts as the ticket.

By train
SKM trains make 16 stops along the coast (from the first station,Gdańsk Śródmieście to Gdynia Główna. The main stops are Gdańsk Główny, Gdańsk Wrzeszcz, Gdańsk Oliwa, Sopot, and Gdynia Główna. Trains depart roughly every 15 minutes and operate from 05:00 to 23:00

A ticket from Gdańsk Śródmieście to Gdynia Główna will cost 9 zł and from Gdańsk Śródmieście to Sopot is 6.40 zł.

By car

In general it is unwise to bring your car to Gdańsk if you plan to spend here longer than a day or two. Parking is expensive, traffic is huge and road quality is quite bad compared to other cities in Poland. Car will be most useful if you wish to visit Stogi or Sobieszewo Island, though parking remains an issue.

Parking spaces in Gdańsk are paid in most of the interesting places including centre of touristy Main City (about 5.50 zł/h entire week 09:00-20:00) and significant parts of upscale districts of Oliwa, Wrzeszcz and remainder of Main City (about 3.90 zł/h, M-F 09:00-17:00). More details can be found on city pages, also a map is available. Parkings in many malls are paid too and are often even more expensive than city parkings. Forum Mall close to the centre will not save you from paying for parking.

Wide roads around Main City are generally hard to navigate for outsiders (think: drive on right lane to turn left), and have huge traffic as they are the only roads available for several thousands of residents of southern Gdańsk who need to pass through them. Forum Mall often creates huge traffic around it, due to wait for parking entrance, clogging several intersections in city centre, including the big one near Śródmieście SKM station.

The main city (Główne Miasto) is the historic part of Gdańsk and contains most of the sights. Drink

Danziger Goldwasser, root and herbal liqueur which has been produced since 16th century is considered the city's drink (it is now made in Germany though). It's vodka-based, creamy and has small flakes of 22- or 23-karat gold in it. Cheaper alternatives include Gdańska Złotówka or Złota Woda.

Gdańsk's national drink before World War II was Stobbes Machandel juniper vodka. After the war it was rejected and slightly forgotten due to association with German soldiers occupying the city, but today is gaining popularity again. There is a special ritual to be followed while drinking a shot of Machandel with a dried plum for a snack.

Stay safe

On the whole, Gdańsk is a safe place to visit and explore. Normal precautions for the safe keeping of your personal possessions, as you would in any other city, are suggested to keep you feel safe when wandering around. Gdańsk seems very well organized from a tourist's point of view. There are frequent police patrols and visitors usually get the feeling of Gdańsk being a secure and tourist-friendly city. 

When it comes to gay, lesbian, and non-white travelers, Gdansk is an oddball compared to the rest of Poland. The city is much more socially progressive and tolerant compared to the rest of the country, and you can be yourself with little to no violent reactions from locals.

Avoid visiting strip clubs, particularly those recommended by individuals in the city center. They usually offer you a free first drink, but there have been reports of drinks being spiked with intoxicants and customers receiving excessively high bills. In recent times, nagging solicitation has increased, and you may receive multiple invitations just while walking along Długa Street.

In an emergency, telephone "112". This number connects to Police, Ambulance and Fire/Rescue services. You will be asked which of these three services you require before being connected to the relevant operator.

On the Hel Peninsula:

Take the train to Hel. It's at the end of the peninsula opposite Gdańsk. SKM trains are the cheaper option. Train from Gdańsk Główny to Gdynia every 15 minutes and then SKM diesel to Hel. If you get the 09:45 from Gdańsk you have 7 minutes to change about 4 platforms across the underpass. The journey is wonderful - straight through pine forests and sometimes with the sea on either side. Single ticket costs 21.50 zł (get a 5.40 zł single from Gdańsk to Gdynia - and then the 16.10 zł single from Gdynia to Hel, just in case you miss the connection.) When you get to Hel have a walk into town, it's a Polish beach resort full of holiday makers. You should have time for lunch and then get the hydrofoil from the end of the pier at 15:30. But make sure you buy the ticket from the office halfway down the pier or you could be walking back! The trip is 50 zł back to Gdańsk but it will be the best value trip you can get. It takes 1hr 50 min but the first hour is getting across to Gdańsk, then the next 50 minutes is navigating the canals back to the town centre. You could pay a tourist company for this trip, but the cost is in your ticket, you see Westphalia lighthouse where the first shots of World War II were fired, the huge ships and the massive remains of the former Lenin Shipyards.
